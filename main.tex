\begin{abstract}
Energy-based models for discrete domains, such as graphs, explicitly capture relative likelihoods, naturally enabling composable probabilistic inference tasks like conditional generation or enforcing constraints at test-time. However, discrete energy-based models typically struggle as generative samplers, as off-support regions often have high-curvature energy landscapes and spurious local minima, trapping samplers and causing training instabilities. This results in a sizable fidelity gap relative to discrete diffusion models, restricting discrete energy-based models predominantly to scoring and uncertainty estimation rather than high-quality sample generation. We introduce \emph{Graph Energy Matching (GEM)}, a generative framework for graphs that closes this fidelity gap. Motivated by the map optimization perspective of the Jordan–Kinderlehrer–Otto formulation, GEM learns a permutation-invariant potential that simultaneously provides transport-aligned guidance from noise toward data and refines samples within regions of high data likelihood. We devised a sampling protocol that leverages an energy-based switch to bridge regimes, seamlessly transitioning from rapid, gradient-guided transport toward high-density regions to rigorous Metropolis–Hastings mixing tailored to the discrete geometry. On molecular graph benchmarks, GEM matches or exceeds strong discrete diffusion baselines. Beyond sample quality alone, the learned potential naturally enables property-constrained generation at test-time, and its explicit relative-likelihood structure  supports further analyses.
\end{abstract}
