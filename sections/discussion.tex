\section{Discussion}
GEM keeps inference-time control compositional by adding new constraints and objectives as energy terms,
and we demonstrate this in the conditional generation experiments (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}). The sampler is
connected to locally balanced schemes through a linearized energy change, but uses gradients rather than
full high-dimensional differences. The explicit energy and relative likelihoods enable new diagnostics,
including geodesic analysis between molecules and HP calibration based on likelihood ratios.

\subsection*{Limitations}
At very small inference budgets, diffusion-based baselines can reach strong performance with fewer steps;
Figure~\ref{fig:moses-vun-steps} shows that in the few-step regime our current GEM sampler trails the
diffusion baseline. More broadly, EBMs for generation rely on gradient-based velocity parameterization,
which typically requires both forward and backward passes per step and implies an inherent $\sim$2--3$\times$
theoretical slowdown relative to purely forward samplers.


\section{Future directions}
GEM naturally extends to 3D molecule generation by using a strong graph prior and composing it with
geometric constraints at inference time, which can improve fidelity and conditional control while
keeping the prior plug-and-play. The energy formulation exposes relative likelihoods that can guide
scalable inference-time refinement and extra constraints without retraining, and the discriminators
are no longer tied to a specific forward process. Advances in equivariant architectures translate
cleanly to energy-based setups through invariant mappings, making the energy itself naturally
invariant. The sampler already overcomes a key bottleneck for energy-based models by delivering
high-quality samples, suggesting room for further scaling and constraint stacking to drive
scientific discovery.
Generalization can be studied through the learned relative likelihoods by holding out subsets of the
dataset and analyzing how energy rankings extrapolate to unseen regions or attribute bands, offering
new diagnostics beyond sample quality alone.

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced GEM, a Graph Energy Matching framework that learns a single explicit potential for
graph generation while directly modeling relative likelihoods. Because control is implemented by
adding energies, GEM accommodates new constraints at inference time without being tied to a fixed
forward process, enabling flexible and compositional conditioning. The resulting sampler achieves
high-quality samples while keeping the model interpretable through its energy landscape.
