\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Generative modeling of discrete structured objects, such as graphs, is central in scientific and industrial
applications (e.g.\ molecular discovery), yet remains challenging due to their combinatorial nature.
Recent progress is dominated by \glspl{dm} and \gls{fm}, which learn to transform a simple
reference distribution (e.g.\ Gaussian or uniform) into the data distribution. In discrete domains, these
methods typically rely on discrete-time diffusion~\citep{vignac2022digress} or continuous-time Markov
chain constructions~\citep{campbell2024generative,qinmadeira2024defog} to handle the non-differentiable state
space. While effective for \emph{unconditional} generation, controlling these models at inference
time can be cumbersome: steering samples toward user-specified property targets typically requires
specialized guidance mechanisms that are tied to the training-time noise process.

Energy-based models (EBMs) provide a complementary viewpoint by representing an unnormalized density
through a single scalar energy~\citep{hinton2002training}. Their main practical appeal is
\emph{compositionality}: constraints, priors, and property objectives can be incorporated at inference
time by adding corresponding energy terms, enabling flexible conditional sampling without retraining
(e.g.\ via energy composition)~\citep{du2019implicit}. On graphs, this perspective is particularly
attractive because constraints and priors can be injected directly into the energy at inference time,
and permutation invariance can be enforced by construction.
However, EBMs have historically underperformed as high-fidelity generators, largely because sampling
is difficult: \gls{mcmc} mixing can be slow in high-dimensional discrete spaces, and energies are poorly
constrained away from observed data. GraphEBM~\citep{liu2021graphebm} lags behind diffusion/flow approaches.
Consequently, energy-based techniques on graphs are frequently used as \emph{scoring} mechanisms for
out-of-distribution detection and uncertainty quantification~\citep{wu2023energy,fuchsgruber2024energy},
rather than as state-of-the-art samplers.

Energy Matching~\citep{balcerak2025energy} bridges these perspectives in continuous spaces by learning a \emph{time-independent}
potential that simultaneously (i) provides guidance for moving samples from a reference
distribution toward the data and (ii) defines a Boltzmann-like density near data via contrastive
refinement. This regularizes energies off-support while retaining the compositional control advantages
of EBMs. Rather than simulating a time-indexed ODE/SDE, we sample directly on the discrete graph space
using local edit proposals inside an \gls{mcmc} chain. This avoids continuous relaxations and matches
the combinatorial nature of graphs. Extending this idea to graphs requires two ingredients: transport
supervision that respects graph symmetries, and discrete sampling mechanisms that are efficient enough
to make energy-based generation competitive.

\Gls{jko} provides a variational framework for optimization in spaces of probability measures, where each
step balances an energy against a distance (or discrepancy) rooted in \gls{ot}, such as the Wasserstein
metric~\citep{terpin2024learning}. This perspective motivates OT-inspired transport supervision given a geometry.
 For graphs, \gls{gw}-type costs offer permutation-respecting
comparisons~\citep{krzakala2024any2graph}. At the same time, computing exact Wasserstein geodesics is
typically impractical at scale. Importantly, stochastic interpolants with data-dependent couplings provide
a principled way to work with tractable, possibly suboptimal couplings: a fixed coupling induces an
admissible transport path whose kinetic action upper-bounds $W_2^2$, yielding a \emph{relaxed
Wasserstein geometry} suitable for learning transport-aligned dynamics without solving exact
OT~\citep{albergo2024stochastic}. This motivates using efficient approximate graph couplings to supervise
global guidance, while relying on Metropolis--Hastings (MH) refinement to ensure a valid energy-based
generative model on the discrete space.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We propose \emph{Graph Energy Matching} (\gls{gem})\footnote{Code will be released upon acceptance.}, a controllable energy-based generative framework for
graphs that combines:
(i) OT-style global guidance from tractable, permutation-aware graph couplings under a relaxed transport
geometry,
(ii) a learned time-independent potential defining an explicit Gibbs model, and
(iii) efficient sampling based on MH updates with geometry-aware discrete proposals.
This yields a practical route to high-quality graph generation while preserving the compositional
advantages of energy-based modeling, enabling inference-time control by adding constraint and objective
energies.
