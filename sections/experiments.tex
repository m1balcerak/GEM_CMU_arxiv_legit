\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We report unconditional generation on MOSES and QM9 in the main text, property optimization under
thresholded targets (logP, logS, QED, TPSA), and geodesic comparisons in the learned space. We report
validity, uniqueness, novelty, $V.U.N.$, and \gls*{fcd} (lower is better), and we consider two
initialization regimes: noise initialization starts from easy-to-sample noise and must reach
chemically valid molecules, while data initialization starts from training molecules and must make
novel yet valid edits (diffusion/CTMC or MCMC). MOSES is a larger scale dataset (about 1.5M molecules),
while QM9 is smaller; we use MOSES for scale and QM9 as a compact comparison.
%
A concise conceptual summary of the baselines used in our experiments is provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}, and the experimental setup is detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:exp-setup}.

\subsection*{Property optimization}
We evaluate property optimization under molecular threshold constraints (above/below a target),
following~\citep{ninniri2025graph}: we train a GraphTransformer regressor \(f_\phi\) for
differentiable guidance and define \(V_\theta^{\mathrm{cond}}(x)=V_\theta(x)+\lambda_{\mathrm{prop}}\|f_\phi(x)-y^\star\|^2\),
then run the same proposal kernel $q_\theta(x\!\to\!y)$ using \(-\nabla_x V_\theta^{\mathrm{cond}}(x)\),
with the Metropolis--Hastings acceptance ratio modified accordingly; for fairness, all methods use
noise-conditioned regressors (GEM clean-regressor ablation in Appendix~\ref{app:regressor-noise},
Figure~\ref{fig:regressor-noise}, Table~\ref{tab:regressor-ablations}) and we feed regressor time via
an energy-based proxy that increases linearly until the chain reaches the data distribution, then
clamps to $t=1$ (Appendix~\ref{app:regressor-noise}). Noise-conditioned regressors are more reliable
on noisy or invalid graphs, and targets (logS $\ge$ -2.25, logP $\le$ 1.5, QED $\ge$ 0.9,
TPSA $\le$ 50) are chosen so unconditional MOSES samples satisfy the constraint at roughly a 20\%
rate, yielding comparable difficulty across properties.
Table~\ref{tab:moses-conditional} reports single-property conditioning results, and
Appendix~\ref{app:baselines} summarizes joint constraints.
GEM maintains strong validity, uniqueness, and novelty while improving constraint satisfaction
relative to baselines.
GEM draws a candidate pool of size $N_{\mathrm{select}}$ from the training set, ranks by the
precomputed property values, and initializes the $B$ inference chains with the top-$B$ molecules
that satisfy the condition; this takes virtually no time because the training-set properties are
cached.

\subsection*{Geodesic analysis}
We compute geodesics between molecules and compare against a linear interpolation baseline.
Shortest paths in high-dimensional spaces have been studied in particular through energy-based
models; see, e.g., \citet{bethune2025follow}.
We plot average validity versus the endpoint distance induced by the matching cost
$c_{\mathrm{FGW}}$ (Appendix~\ref{app:matching}), comparing energy-weighted geodesics to cost-only
baselines; see Figure~\ref{fig:geodesic-validity}. Qualitative paths are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:mol-trajectories}.
Energy-weighted geodesics consistently preserve validity relative to cost-only geodesics, confirming
that the learned energy captures chemically meaningful geometry. Full protocol, pairing,
optimization, and discretization details are in Appendix~\ref{app:geodesic-details}.
