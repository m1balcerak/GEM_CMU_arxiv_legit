\section{Preliminaries}

\paragraph{Energy-based models and sampling challenges.}
An \gls{ebm} assigns an energy $E_\theta(x)$, inducing $\pi_\theta(x)\propto \exp(-E_\theta(x))$.
Maximum-likelihood training uses contrastive objectives with negatives from \gls{mcmc}; the energy is
poorly posed outside the support of the target distribution and chains can mix slowly. The energy view
is compositional in the sense that independent factors in the posterior correspond to additive energy terms:
$p(x \mid y)\propto \underbrace{p(y \mid x)}_{\text{conditional likelihood}}\underbrace{p(x)}_{\text{prior}}$,
where $y$ is an observation/measurement and $x$ is a sample. The conditional term is often provided by
a discriminator/regressor; a common Gaussian data-fidelity choice is
$p(y\mid x)\propto \exp(-\lVert y - A(x)\rVert^2/\zeta^2)$, where $A(x)$ is a forward/measurement model
and $\zeta$ is a scalar controlling adherence to the condition. At inference, posterior sampling is
obtained by adding the data-fidelity term (and any other constraint energies) to $E_\theta$.

\paragraph{Optimal transport costs, stochastic interpolants, and kinetic action.}
Given a cost $c:\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{X}\to[0,\infty)$ and distributions $\rho_0,\rho_1$, the
Kantorovich transport cost is
\begin{equation}
W_c(\rho_0,\rho_1)
\;\coloneqq\;
\inf_{\gamma\in\Gamma(\rho_0,\rho_1)} \mathbb{E}_\gamma[c(X_0,X_1)],
\end{equation}
with $\Gamma(\rho_0,\rho_1)$ the set of couplings. For $\mathcal{X}=\mathbb{R}^d$ and
$c(x_0,x_1)=\|x_0-x_1\|^2$, $W_c=W_2^2$. Exact OT couplings are typically expensive to compute, but for
any coupling the induced kinetic action can be evaluated as a discrepancy that upper-bounds the true
transport cost and provides a usable geometry. Fixing a coupling $\gamma$ with $(X_0,X_1)\sim\gamma$,
define the interpolant $I_t=(1-t)X_0+tX_1$ and velocity field
$b_t^\gamma(x)\coloneqq\mathbb{E}[X_1-X_0\mid I_t=x]$, which induce the kinetic action
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kinetic-action}
\mathcal{A}(\gamma)
\;\coloneqq\;
\int_0^1 \int \|b_t^\gamma(x)\|^2\,\rho_t^\gamma(\mathrm{d}x)\,\mathrm{d}t.
\end{equation}
For quadratic cost,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kinetic-action-bound}
W_2^2(\rho_0,\rho_1)\le\mathcal{A}(\gamma)\le\mathbb{E}_\gamma[\|X_1-X_0\|^2],
\end{equation}
with equality for optimal deterministic (Monge) couplings. A tractable coupling
(e.g.\ minibatch matching) is therefore used, and $\mathcal{A}(\gamma)$ is treated as a restricted
geometry that upper-bounds the true Wasserstein distance.

\paragraph{Energy matching and a learned potential.}
Energy matching learns a scalar \emph{potential} $V_\theta:\mathcal{X}\to\mathbb{R}$ with two roles.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Off-manifold guidance:} away from the data distribution, the vector field $-\nabla V_\theta$
is trained to align with transport-like directions that move samples from a simple source distribution
toward the data.
\item \textbf{Near-equilibrium refinement:} near equilibrium, $V_\theta$ acts as an EBM energy and
defines a Gibbs density with unit temperature.
\end{itemize}
This separates learning into a global transport-alignment term and a local density refinement term.
A two-regime temperature $\varepsilon(\cdot)$ is used: $\varepsilon=0$ off-manifold (transport regime)
and $\varepsilon=1$ near equilibrium (mixing regime). This regime switch injects exploration, helps prevent mode
collapse, and stabilizes the Gibbs distribution in the mixing regime.
In continuous space, a JKO step for the energy-and-entropy functional reads
\begin{align}
\rho_{t+\Delta t}
&=
\arg\min_{\rho}\;
\frac{1}{2\Delta t}\,W_2^2(\rho,\rho_t)\nonumber\\
&\quad+\;\int V_\theta(x)\,\rho(x)\,\mathrm{d}x\nonumber\\
&\quad+\;\varepsilon(t)\int \rho(x)\log\rho(x)\,\mathrm{d}x.
\end{align}

These first-order optimality conditions in Wasserstein space~\citep{lanzetti2025firstorder,lanzetti2024variational} motivate two objectives~\citep{balcerak2025energy}:
away from data, transport directions are matched; near equilibrium, the energy is refined to recover the Gibbs distribution.
Given optimally paired endpoints $(X_0,X_1)\sim\gamma$ and $t\sim\Unif([0,1])$, define $X_t=(1-t)X_0+tX_1$ and
$v=X_1-X_0$. The potential $V_\theta$ is trained by matching the gradient field to $v$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OT}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{(X_0,X_1)\sim\gamma,\;t\sim\Unif([0,1])}
\big[\|-\nabla_x V_\theta(X_t) - v\|^2\big].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Energy refinement (near-equilibrium).}
As $t\to\infty$, the equilibrium distribution is
\begin{equation}
p_{t\to\infty}(x)\;\propto\;\exp\!\left(-V_\theta(x)\right).
\end{equation}
The refinement of $p_{t\to\infty}$ uses
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ER}}
=
\mathbb{E}_{x^{+}\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}}\!\left[V_\theta(x^{+})\right]
-
\mathbb{E}_{x^{-}\sim \mathrm{sg}(p_{t\to\infty})}\!\left[V_\theta(x^{-})\right],
\end{equation}
where $x^{-}$ are sampled from the current chain (stop-gradient through the sampler). Chains are
initialized from the source or from data with a tunable mixing probability. A warm-start with
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OT}}$ allows source-initialized chains to reach low-energy regions, followed by
optimization of
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OT}}(\theta)+\lambda_{\mathrm{ER}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ER}}(\theta).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Sampling (two regimes).}
Sampling interpolates between transport-aligned drift and mixing via an effective temperature
$\varepsilon(t)$. In continuous time this is the time-inhomogeneous SDE
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{d}X_t
=
-\nabla_x V_\theta(X_t)\,\mathrm{d}t
+\sqrt{2\,\varepsilon(t)}\,\mathrm{d}W_t,
\end{equation}
which reduces to an ODE when $\varepsilon(t)=0$ (off-manifold) and becomes a stochastic dynamics as
the regime switches and $\varepsilon(t)\to 1$. In practice this dynamics needs to be
integrated over time.

\paragraph{Related work: discrete diffusion/flow matching and control.}
Recent progress in discrete graph generation is dominated by denoising diffusion and CTMC
formulations. DiGress~\citep{vignac2022digress} extends discrete diffusion to categorical node and edge
attributes, while discrete CTMCs~\citep{campbell2024generative} move to a continuous-time graph diffusion process. Discrete
Flow Models (DFMs) show that flow-like probability paths on discrete spaces can be realized via
CTMCs~\citep{campbell2024generative}, and DeFoG~\citep{qinmadeira2024defog} instantiates this discrete
flow-matching viewpoint for graphs, emphasizing a more flexible separation between training and
sampling. Variational Flow Matching (VFM) recasts flow matching as variational inference over
trajectory endpoints, and CatFlow applies this to categorical data with strong results on graphs~\citep{eijkelboom2024vfm}.
For controllable generation, diffusion/CTMC guidance typically assumes access to a time- or
noise-dependent conditional signal along the path (e.g., a regressor/classifier for $p(y\mid x_t,t)$ or
$p_t(y\mid x_t)$). In discrete settings this often requires training a dedicated predictor on noised
latents across noise levels (or separate conditional/unconditional models for CFG), adding extra
training and coupling the control mechanism to the chosen noising process~\citep{schiff2024discreteguidance,eijkelboom2025controlledvfm}.
Moreover, property prediction on heavily corrupted graphs is ill-posed because bonds and valences are
often invalid, so regressor accuracy naturally degrades away from clean samples; we quantify this
effect in Appendix~\ref{app:regressor-noise}.
A concise conceptual summary of the baselines used in our experiments is provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}.
